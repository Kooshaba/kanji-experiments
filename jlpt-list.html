Kanji by JLPT level

Kanji by JLPT level

The Japanese-Language Proficiency Test (
), usually abbreviated to JLPT, is the main standardized test of Japanese ability for non-native speakers.
The JLPT is divided into 5 levels, known as N5 through N1. N5 is the lowest (easiest) level, and is thought to take 250-400 hours of study. The highest level, N1, is expected to take students with no prior kanji knowledge about 3100-4500 hours of study. Every year, the test is taken by about 500-750,000 people around the world, with a pass rate of about 70% for N5, and about 35% for N1.
The official stance of the JLPT administrators is that there are no kanji or vocab lists for the JLPT, as studying from lists is discouraged. As a result, it is not possible to be certain which kanji or words will or will not appear in a given test. However, in practice, the kanji and words used by level do follow consistent patterns. Below we present Kanshudo's own assessment of the key kanji to learn for each level. Our lists were developed based on our own assessment of past tests, and comparison with other available lists of JLPT kanji.
Our lists are broken down as follows:
  • N5 - 80 kanji
  • N4 - 170 kanji
  • N3 - 370 kanji
  • N2 - 380 kanji
  • N1 - 1136 kanji
  • Total = 2136 (the full list of Jy Kanji)
Our analysis of JLPT level also forms the basis of the 'rings' used in our Kanji Mastery system. N5 corresponds to ring 2 (ring 1 is the hiragana/katakana), through to N1 which is ring 6 (ring 7 is 'everything else'). So studying the kanji in these collections is equivalent to studying kanji by ring from your personalized kanji wheel.
To study the kanji used in N5, start with our popular series of Beginner Lessons.
Below we present the kanji for each JLPT level ordered by frequency of use in daily life, and broken into smaller groups of around 100 kanji each for ease of studying.
For each set, you can use the links to:
  • Study immediately with Quick Study
  • Create Flashcards for study any time with our spaced repetition flashcard system
  • View your current Kanji Mastery level for each kanji, as well as a quick summary of readings and meanings
  • Download the kanji with readings and meanings for study offline or in another flashcard program
You can also click on any kanji to view its full details and practice drawing it.
For more information, see:
  • Japanese-Language Proficiency Test (Wikipedia)
  • JLPT Japanese-Language Proficiency Test (main home page)

The pitch accent markings show you how to pronounce a word. A line underneath a kana symbol indicates low pitch, and a line above indicates high pitch. A vertical line at the end indicates the pitch of the next sound in a sentence using the word is different to the last sound of the word. The number(s) to the right of the markings show how this pitch is represented in a Japanese dictionary.
